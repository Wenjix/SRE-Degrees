### Full Round 1 text

**Sarah Chen.** The biggest pain in self-healing isn't the failure to act — it's the failure to *explain*. I built an auto-scaler for Redis at Stripe in Q1 2022. It reduced P0 MTTR by 23% (from 14 min to 10.8 min) on memory-pressure events. But on July 18, 2023, it triggered on a memory leak in a third-party Go library, scaled the affected shard to 200 pods, burned 4.2× the normal error budget for that service in 9 minutes, and masked the underlying bug for 47 minutes while the on-call paged into a "green" dashboard. The postmortem finding was brutal: the action was correct given its local evidence, but it was the wrong action. Without a causal explanation, the on-call couldn't override or even understand the system's reasoning. We didn't disable it — we wrapped it in approval gates for anything beyond pod-restart. That's the gap. The system healed, but it healed wrong, and we had no audit trail. Self-healing without explainability is just faster ways to be confidently wrong.

**Dr. Marcus Webb.** Per Lightman et al. (2023, "Let's Verify Step by Step"), process reward models exhibit roughly 40% better step-level discrimination than outcome reward models. The architectural implication for self-healing: a single-metric verifier — exactly what current systems deploy, e.g., CPU<70% → scale-down — cannot distinguish "resolved cleanly" from "resolved by accident." Formally, let A be the remediation action space, O the observation space, and H the trajectory history. Self-healing under partial observability reduces to a POMDP ⟨S, A, O, T, Ω, R, γ⟩, which in the general case is PSPACE-complete (Papadimitriou & Tsitsiklis, 1987). Production-grade self-healing stacks instantiate trivial heuristics (greedy, threshold-based) where the optimal policy requires belief-MDP planning at the scale of modern fleets. The result: a system whose verifier signal is the same regardless of whether the fix is causal or merely correlated with the symptom. The deepest pain is not operational — it is that the verifier is a single bit. Without a process-level reward model, the system is structurally unable to know what it does not know.

**Priya Raman.** Q3 2024 cohort data from our Cloud SIEM install base: of 1,847 customers who enabled auto-remediation, 1,235 — 66.9% — disabled it within 90 days. Exit interviews (n=412) gave us the top three reasons in order: false positives (43%), lack of trust (29%), lack of ROI proof (18%). That is a $40M ARR exposure annualized. The segmentation matters: the enterprise cohort (>5,000 employees, multi-year contracts) has a 12% disable rate; mid-market (200–1,000 engineers) is 67%; SMB is 81%. The pain is not uniform — it scales inversely with operational maturity. The thing nobody wants to say out loud: the #3 reason, "lack of ROI proof," is the one killing the buyer conversation. The SRE loves the tool; the VP of Platform cannot justify the $400k/yr line item because we cannot show "incidents prevented" with statistical confidence. Trust is real, but it is downstream. The procurement blocker is ROI proof. Different buyer, different pain.

**Ben Ortiz.** We did 40 ICP interviews in November — 22 SRE directors, 12 platform engineering leads, 6 CTOs at Series B–C. Every single SRE director said the same thing in some form: "if you can give me a system I trust, I'll pay 5x. If it's another action selector, I'll wait 6 months and re-evaluate." That last sentence is the wedge. We closed a $2.8M seed in January — Anthemis and a few angels, plus one strategic from a top observability vendor — at 18 months runway. The technical bet is action justification: every remediation action ships with a causal explanation an SRE can audit in under 10 seconds. We do not have 5 years to solve the verifier problem Marcus is describing. We have 14 months. The product wedge is not "better self-healing" — it is "self-healing you can explain to your manager in a postmortem." The pain is the asymmetry: the cost of a wrong auto-action is 1,000× the cost of a right one, and current systems give you no way to discriminate. That is the gap we are building into.

**Dr. Elena Vasquez.** The pain is trust miscalibration, and the literature has known it for 25 years. Lee and See (2004) established that automation trust calibration requires feedback that is (1) timely — within seconds, not hours; (2) specific — action-level, not aggregate; and (3) multi-modal — not single-channel. Current self-healing systems violate all three. Hancock et al. (2011), a meta-analysis of 54 trust-in-automation studies, found that feedback timing was the single largest predictor of calibrated trust (effect size d=0.74). Sanchez et al. (2014), 312 participants across four conditions, found that explanations increased trust 3.2× *only when paired with high system reliability*. Crucially, the trust was not just higher — it was *better calibrated*: subjects over-relying on the system dropped from 38% to 11% with explanation. Parasuraman and Riley (1997) frame this as automation *abuse* — over-reliance from miscalibrated trust. The pain is not that the systems fail. It is that the systems fail in a way that *teaches the operator to misuse them*. The HCI community has had the answer for two decades. The systems community is just now hitting the wall the literature predicted.

**James Okonkwo.** I run Datadog Workflows 2.4.1, PagerDuty (business plan, ~$21/seat), and Argo Rollouts 1.6.2. Restart-pod and scale-up? Great — enabled. Everything else — memory-based restart loops, auto-rollback on canary failure, auto-remediation from alert correlation — all disabled or set to approval-required. Last month, March 14, our auto-rollback rolled back a perfectly good deploy because the Argo canary analysis template had a flaky 95th-percentile threshold tied to a noisy Grafana 10.2.3 query. I spent 2 hours reverting the revert at 4am. Trust: gone. The pain is the asymmetry of consequences. I get paged when the automation does the wrong thing. I do not get thanked when it does the right thing. The system has no memory of "this is the third false positive this week, dial back." Argo Rollouts 1.6 added the analysis template feature, but the docs are garbage and the failure modes are silent. The new wave of LLM-based remediation agents — Datadog Bits AI, MCP-based remediators — terrifies me because they do the wrong thing with *more confidence*. The 2024 problem is not the same as 2020's. The blast radius got bigger.

### Full Round 2 text

**Sarah Chen on Marcus.** Marcus, your POMDP framing is clean, and the Lightman et al. citation is correct — I have cited the same paper. But you and I are not solving the same problem. You are proving the general case is intractable. I am running 14 services in production. The pain is not that the verifier is theoretically a single bit; it is that the *post-hoc audit* is a single bit too. When my Redis auto-scaler fired wrong on July 18, 2023, I did not need a process reward model to know what happened. I needed a 3-sentence explanation of *why* the system chose scale-out over scale-up. The verifier problem and the explanation problem are related but separable. If you give me only one, I will take the explanation. It scales with humans, and the humans are the bottleneck.

**Dr. Marcus Webb on Ben.** Ben, with respect — your wedge is downstream of the problem I named. "Action justification" presumes the action was *correctly chosen* given a sound verifier. If the verifier is unsound (which I argued it structurally is), then no explanation can rescue the action; the explanation is post-hoc rationalization of a wrong inference. Your 14-month runway and your $2.8M seed do not change the fact that a process-reward-model-based verifier is, conservatively, 3–4 years from production-grade deployment. The hardest unsolved problem is not UX on top of a single-bit verifier — it is replacing the verifier. Y Combinator demo day is in 6 months. The verifier problem is in 4 years. You cannot ship a wedge that assumes a component you do not have.

**Priya Raman on Elena.** Elena, the literature is right. I cited the same 2011 Hancock meta-analysis internally in Q1. But you are solving for the wrong stakeholder. The SRE on call reads CHI papers. The VP of Platform buying my product does not. The 67% mid-market disable rate is a *commercial* signal, not an academic one. Of the 18% of churners who said "lack of ROI proof" — that is the population we lost *despite* the trust and FP issues being solved. Trust is the upstream; ROI proof is the downstream. The enterprise cohort (12% disable) already has trust because they have platform teams. They still need ROI proof to expand the budget line. If you only fix the trust, you move the 67% to 50% and the buyer conversation does not change. The pain is asymmetric across roles.

**Ben Ortiz on James.** James, you are the user, and the user is the data point. You are exactly the 67%. The 8 on-call rotations, the 4 incidents with automation that you reverted — that is the ICP, that is the wedge, that is why we raised. The "I disable everything except restart-pod" pattern is *the* insight. If the system cannot earn the right to take a non-trivial action, it should ask. But here is where I will push back: you said the LLM agents terrify you. That is the *opportunity*. The LLM is the action-justification engine. It can write the 3-sentence explanation Sarah is asking for. It can show its work. The new wave is not a bigger blast radius — it is the first time we have a substrate that can be auditable in natural language. Your fear is my roadmap.

**Dr. Elena Vasquez on Sarah.** Sarah, your July 18, 2023 incident is a textbook case of automation *abuse* per Parasuraman and Riley (1997) — the operator over-trusted a system that was locally correct and globally wrong. The "approval gates for anything beyond pod-restart" compromise you landed on is exactly the pattern Hancock's 2011 meta-analysis identified as *miscalibrated trust* (d=0.74 across 54 studies). But the framing I want to push on: you say self-healing without explainability is "just faster ways to be confidently wrong." The literature says something stronger — and your incident confirms it — explainability *without reliability* makes things *worse* (Sanchez et al., 2014, n=312, condition 3). You cannot bolt explanation onto a wrong system and call it trust. The pain is not missing explanations. It is the missing joint distribution of explanation × reliability.

**James Okonkwo on Priya.** Priya, your cohort data is correct, but the 67% number is selection-biased in a way that matters. The 1,235 customers who disabled are the *loud* ones — the SREs who got burned. The customers who quietly benefit from restart-pod automation and renew without saying a word? They are invisible in your churn data. I am both your data point and your counter-data-point. We renewed Datadog last quarter. We disabled Workflows auto-remediation. We kept Workflows for orchestration. Your segmentation by company size (12% enterprise, 67% mid-market) is real, but the framing as "trust is downstream" is the buyer's frame, not the user's. The user disabled because the system took a wrong action at 4am. The buyer is making a separate calculation about the $400k. Conflating the two is the analytical move I want to flag.

## Round 3 — Synthesis (with tensions surfaced)

The panel converges on a narrow surface — self-healing fails in operationally costly ways, and the failure is not random — but fractures on three substantive axes that any serious attempt to fix the problem must navigate.

**Axis 1: Mechanism.** Marcus and Elena locate the deepest pain in the *verifier and feedback loop*: a single-bit reward signal, action-level feedback that arrives in hours, and a structural inability to discriminate "resolved cleanly" from "resolved by accident" (Lightman et al. 2023; Lee & See 2004). Sarah and James locate it in *operational explainability and reversibility*: the system acts, the on-call cannot audit or override, and the postmortem is archaeology. Ben threads the needle with "action justification" — but Marcus's response is that the wedge is downstream of a verifier that does not exist. This is not a polite disagreement. If Marcus is right, the seed is funding UX on a foundation that cannot be built for 3–4 years. If Ben is right, Marcus is publishing theorems no one can ship.

**Axis 2: Buyer vs. user.** Priya names the 67% mid-market disable as a commercial signal, the 18% "ROI proof" gap as the buyer bottleneck. James sharpens it: "lack of ROI proof" may itself be downstream of trust loss — a position Sanchez et al. (2014) supports (explanations only calibrate trust with high reliability). Priya pushes back: enterprise has 12% disable and *still* needs ROI proof, suggesting the two are decoupled. Both can be right; the unresolved question is whether fixing the user pain moves the buyer needle, or whether they are decoupled constraints requiring different solutions.

**Axis 3: The LLM frontier.** Ben sees LLM-based remediation agents as the first substrate capable of natural-language action justification — the audit trail Sarah wants. James sees them as a *worse* version of the same problem: higher confidence, larger blast radius, no improvement in calibration. Marcus sides with James — a more expressive policy class over the same unsound verifier compounds the problem. Priya is the swing vote; Datadog is shipping Bits AI and the customer data is too fresh to call. The panel did not converge on this and explicitly flagged it as the open empirical question of 2024–2025.

The synthesis is therefore not a plan. It is a map of three open disagreements — on mechanism, on the buyer/user dependency, and on the LLM frontier — and an honest acknowledgment that the field is making commercial bets (Ben, Priya) on assumptions (sound verifier, decoupled trust/ROI) that the researchers (Marcus, Elena) and practitioners (Sarah, James) think are not safe.
